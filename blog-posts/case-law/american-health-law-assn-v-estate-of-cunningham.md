# Appellate Court Dismisses Arbitration Organization's Interlocutory
Appeal of Immunity Denial, Holding That Statutory Immunity from Civil
Damages Is Immunity from Liability---Not Immunity from Suit---and
Therefore Not Immediately Appealable

**Court:** Court of Appeals of Arkansas (Appeal from the Bradley County Circuit
Court, No. 06CV-22-11, Hon. Robert Bynum Gibson III, Judge)
**Date:** March 19, 2025 --- 2025 Ark. App. 171, 2025 WL 852768

---

## Introduction

The American Health Law Association (AHLA), a professional membership
association of health law professionals, was sued by the Estate of Fred
Cunningham. (Note: The case caption references AHLA, but the underlying
statute concerns arbitration organization immunity. The party's precise
role should be verified against the full court opinion.) The underlying
dispute involved a negligence claim brought by the Estate against AHLA.
The specific facts of the underlying negligence claim are not detailed
in the appellate opinion, as the court addressed only the procedural
question of appealability. The central controversy was whether the circuit court's denial of AHLA's immunity-based summary judgment motion was immediately appealable.

## The Procedural Background

The Estate filed a complaint and amended complaint against AHLA. AHLA
filed a motion to dismiss claiming immunity, which the Bradley County
Circuit Court denied on April 14, 2023. No appeal was taken from that
order. On June 20, 2023, AHLA moved for summary judgment arguing the
Estate could not establish a negligence claim because AHLA is immune
from damages under Ark. Code Ann. § 16-108-241(a). The circuit court
denied summary judgment on September 15, 2023. AHLA appealed the denial. The Estate moved to dismiss the appeal for want of a final order.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

The central controversy was whether the circuit court's order denying
AHLA's summary judgment motion based on statutory immunity constituted a
final, appealable order. This required the court to determine whether
Ark. Code Ann. § 16-108-241(a) confers immunity from suit (which would
be immediately appealable) or merely immunity from liability (which
would not).

## Arguments Presented to the Court

AHLA (Appellant): Argued it was immune from civil damages under the
arbitration organization immunity statute and that the denial of its
summary judgment motion on immunity grounds was immediately appealable.

Estate of Cunningham (Appellee): Moved to dismiss the appeal,
arguing that the denial of summary judgment based on immunity from
liability (as opposed to sovereign immunity or immunity from suit) is
not a final, appealable order.

## The Court's Holding

Motion to dismiss granted; appeal dismissed without prejudice. The
appellate court held it lacked jurisdiction because the circuit court's
order was not a final, appealable order.

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

The court relied on Baptist Health v. Sourinphoumy, 2023 Ark. 15, which
distinguished between immunity from suit and immunity from liability.
Immunity from suit is the entitlement not to stand trial, while immunity
from liability is merely a defense. Interlocutory appeals are permitted
only for sovereign immunity or immunity of a government official because
the right to immunity from suit is effectively lost if the case goes to
trial. Here, Ark. Code Ann. § 16-108-241(a) provides that an arbitration
organization "is immune from civil damages"---which is immunity from
liability, not immunity from suit. Because the immunity at issue is one
of liability, the general rule that an appeal may not be taken from an
order denying a motion to dismiss applied.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision is essential for practitioners asserting statutory immunity defenses because it draws a sharp line between immunity from suit and immunity from liability. A statute that shields a party from "civil damages" confers only immunity from liability---a defense to be resolved at trial---not immunity from suit that can be vindicated through an interlocutory appeal. Mischaracterizing the type of immunity can result in a dismissed appeal and wasted resources.

Key practice points include:
- Before filing an interlocutory appeal from a denied immunity motion, carefully analyze whether the statute confers immunity from suit (right not to stand trial) or immunity from liability (defense on the merits)
- Only sovereign immunity and immunity of government officials---true immunities from suit---support immediate interlocutory appeal under the collateral order doctrine
- Practitioners relying on statutory immunity provisions that reference "damages" or "liability" rather than "suit" should plan for a full trial rather than expecting early appellate relief

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of Arkansas. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
