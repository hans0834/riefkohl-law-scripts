This West Virginia appellate decision addresses a critical issue in property law: what happens when one cotenant claims to have adversely possessed another cotenant's mineral rights. The court held that the claiming party must show actual notice of ouster was given to the dispossessed cotenant, raising the bar for adverse possession claims in shared mineral estates.

# West Virginia Intermediate Court of Appeals Reverses Summary Judgment,
Holding That Adverse Possession Claim Between Cotenants of Mineral
Rights Requires Showing of Actual Notice of Ouster

**Court:** West Virginia Intermediate Court of Appeals (Appeal from Tyler County
Circuit Court)
**Date:** March 19, 2025 --- 2025 WL 854732

---

## Introduction

The dispute concerned ownership of surface and oil/gas estates in
approximately 97 acres in Tyler County, West Virginia. The property had
a complex ownership history dating to the late 1800s, with various
conveyances, straw-party transactions creating joint tenancies, oil/gas
leases from the 1950s, and multiple subsequent transfers. In 2014,
Antero acquired an interest in a 1976 oil and gas lease and in
2016--2017 drilled five producing horizontal wells. The Pike family
(descendants of original owners through the Fordyce line) claimed an
interest in the mineral rights. The case raised critical questions about the standard for adverse possession between cotenants of mineral estates and the notice requirements that must be satisfied.

## The Procedural Background

In September 2019, the Pike Respondents filed a federal
trespass/conversion lawsuit. Also in September 2019, Antero filed a
state declaratory judgment action in Tyler County Circuit Court. In
November 2022, the circuit court initially denied both parties' motions
for summary judgment. On February 27, 2024, the circuit court granted
the Pike Respondents' motion for summary judgment on Antero's adverse
possession claim and all other claims. Antero appealed.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

(1) Whether the circuit court erred in holding the Pike Respondents'
mineral interest was not adversely possessed; (2) whether Antero must
demonstrate actual notice of ouster to the Pike family (as cotenants);
and (3) whether the property was adversely possessed for the statutory
ten-year period.

## Arguments Presented to the Court

Antero (Petitioner): Argued that non-Pike parties adversely
possessed the Pike interests through open and notorious possession for
more than ten years, extinguishing the Pike family's claims.

Pike Respondents: Argued that as cotenants, they could not be ousted
without actual notice, and that no such ouster was demonstrated.

## The Court's Holding

West Virginia Intermediate Court of Appeals Reverses Summary Judgment,
Holding That Adverse Possession Claim Between Cotenants of Mineral
Rights Requires Showing of Actual Notice of Ouster

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

Under West Virginia law, when one cotenant claims adverse possession
from another, the statute of limitations does not begin to run until
there is actual ouster---an express denial of the other cotenant's title
and right to possession---brought home openly and unequivocally. The
circuit court erred in two ways: first, by granting summary judgment for
the Pike Respondents without properly analyzing whether actual notice of
ouster was shown; and second, in its analysis of whether ten years of
adverse possession had been established. The court found that there were
genuine issues of material fact regarding when and whether an ouster
occurred and whether the Pike family had actual notice.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision has significant implications for mineral rights disputes, particularly in states following the cotenant ouster doctrine. The court clarified that adverse possession between cotenants of mineral estates requires proof of actual notice of ouster---not merely constructive notice through recording deeds or paying taxes. This heightened standard protects mineral interest holders from losing their rights without clear, direct knowledge that a co-owner is claiming exclusive ownership.

Key practice points include:
- Adverse possession claims between cotenants require proof that the dispossessed cotenant received actual notice of an express denial of their title and right to possession
- Merely recording deeds, paying taxes, or entering into oil and gas leases does not constitute the type of open and unequivocal ouster required between cotenants
- In mineral rights disputes with complex multi-generational ownership histories, practitioners should conduct thorough title searches and be prepared to demonstrate (or rebut) actual notice through direct evidence rather than circumstantial chain-of-title arguments

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of West Virginia. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
