# Maryland Appellate Court Affirms Summary Judgment for Real Estate Agents
and Companies in Property Fraud Case, Finding No Duty to Disclose Septic
System Defect and Upholding Contractual Fee Award

**Court:** Appellate Court of Maryland (Appeal from the Circuit Court, unreported
opinion)
**Date:** September 2, 2025 --- 2025 WL 2525713 (Circuit Court Judgment: February
1, 2022)

---

## Introduction

In July 2017, Harrison Chaires entered a residential contract for a
Maryland property. The contract included an "AS IS" Addendum stating the
property was sold in "AS IS" condition, with the buyer entitled to
inspect at buyer's expense and to terminate within 15 days if
dissatisfied. The seller was represented by Bridget Scott of Innovative
Properties; the buyer was represented by David Wright of Champion
Realty. After settlement in 2019, Chaires discovered the septic system
had failed inspection. The case raised questions about which real estate agent bears the duty to disclose property defects and whether a forfeited corporate charter bars litigation participation.

## The Procedural Background

In July 2020, Chaires filed a complaint alleging concealment, deceit,
negligent misrepresentation, conspiracy to deceive, constructive fraud,
and breach of fiduciary duty against Champion Realty, Scott, Innovative
Properties, and others. In June 2021, Innovative and Scott filed a
motion to dismiss/motion for summary judgment. The circuit court granted
summary judgment on all counts in February 2022. The court subsequently
awarded \$49,827.50 in attorneys' fees. Chaires appealed both the summary judgment and the fee award.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

(1) Whether defendants were precluded from seeking relief due to a
forfeited corporate charter; (2) whether the seller's agent (Scott) owed
a duty to disclose the septic system condition to the buyer; and (3)
whether the attorneys' fees award was proper.

## Arguments Presented to the Court

Chaires (Appellant): Argued that Scott and Innovative Properties
owed him a duty to disclose the septic system defect, that the forfeited
corporate charter barred defendants from relief, and that the fee award
was improper.

Defendants (Appellees): Argued that only the buyer's own broker owed
a duty to disclose under Maryland law, that a forfeited corporation may
still participate in litigation to resolve liabilities, and that the
contract entitled them to fees as prevailing parties.

## The Court's Holding

Affirmed in all respects.

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

The court held: (1) Under Md. Code Ann. § 3-515(c), a forfeited
corporation continues existing for limited purposes and can participate
in litigation. (2) Under Md. Real Property § 10-702(m), the buyer's broker has a duty
to inform the purchaser of the purchaser's rights and obligations under
the property disclosure provisions of the statute; this duty runs to the
buyer's broker, not the seller's agent, and Scott therefore owed no such
duty to the buyer. All remaining
claims---fraudulent concealment, constructive fraud, breach of fiduciary
duty---required a predicate duty that did not exist between Scott and
the buyer. (3) Contract Paragraph 36 provided indemnification including
fees for any action based on contract performance; defendants were
entitled to fees as prevailing parties.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision clarifies the allocation of statutory disclosure duties in Maryland real estate transactions. Under Md. Real Property section 10-702(m), the buyer's broker has a duty to inform the purchaser of the purchaser's rights and obligations under the property disclosure provisions of the statute. The seller's agent has no such obligation to the buyer. This distinction is critical for practitioners handling property fraud or concealment claims, as all derivative causes of action (fraudulent concealment, constructive fraud, breach of fiduciary duty) fail without a predicate duty between the defendant agent and the buyer.

Key practice points include:
- In Maryland, buyers who discover undisclosed property defects after settlement should evaluate claims against their own broker rather than the seller's agent, since section 10-702(m) places the duty to inform the purchaser of disclosure rights and obligations on the buyer's broker
- An "AS IS" addendum with a buyer inspection contingency places the burden on the buyer to discover defects before closing, limiting post-settlement remedies
- Indemnification clauses in real estate contracts may expose unsuccessful plaintiffs to substantial attorneys' fee awards, making pre-litigation assessment of the applicable duty framework essential

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of Maryland. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
