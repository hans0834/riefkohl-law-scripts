# Massachusetts Appeals Court Affirms Conviction for Violation of Abuse
Prevention Order Where Defendant Mailed Letter to Protected Party in
Family Court Dispute

**Court:** Appeals Court of Massachusetts
**Date:** August 12, 2025 --- 2025 WL 2317395

---

## Introduction

Anthony M. Valenti and the victim had a dating relationship from 2021 to
2023 and had a child together in 2022. The victim obtained an abuse
prevention order on May 8, 2023, prohibiting Valenti from contacting
her. The victim retained counsel in a Probate and Family Court matter.
Valenti mailed a letter directly to the victim containing threats about
what he wanted in the probate court proceedings, in violation of the
no-contact order. The case raised the question of whether the evidence was sufficient to sustain a conviction for violating the no-contact order and whether the defendant received effective assistance of counsel.

## The Procedural Background

Valenti was tried in a jury-waived trial in District Court and convicted
of violating the abuse prevention order under G.L. c. 209A, § 7. He appealed pro se.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

(1) Whether the evidence was sufficient to sustain the conviction; and
(2) whether Valenti received ineffective assistance of counsel.

## Arguments Presented to the Court

Valenti (Defendant/Appellant): Argued the evidence was insufficient
and claimed ineffective assistance of counsel. Asserted he was merely
exercising his legal rights in the probate court proceeding.

Commonwealth (Prosecution): Argued the evidence clearly established
a valid no-contact order, Valenti's knowledge of it, and his violation
by mailing the letter directly to the protected party.

## The Court's Holding

Affirmed.

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

To establish a violation of a § 209A order, the Commonwealth must prove:
(1) a valid order in effect; (2) the defendant's knowledge of it; (3) a
violation. Valenti did not contest the first two elements. The violation
was established by the mailing. No-contact orders impose obligations
regardless of the defendant's intent or claimed legal purpose. The judge
found Valenti not credible, and credibility determinations are deferred
to the trial judge. The ineffective assistance claim lacked merit
because Valenti did not specify what arguments were confusing or what
evidence went unintroduced.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision confirms that abuse prevention no-contact orders are absolute prohibitions. A defendant cannot justify direct communication with a protected party by claiming a legal purpose related to pending family court proceedings. The proper channel for any communication is through counsel, not direct contact. Practitioners representing parties subject to protective orders must ensure their clients understand that no exception exists for "legal" communications.

Key practice points include:
- No-contact abuse prevention orders under G.L. c. 209A prohibit all direct communication regardless of the defendant's stated purpose, including communications related to pending family court matters
- Defendants in family court disputes who are subject to no-contact orders must communicate exclusively through counsel; direct correspondence constitutes a violation even if the content relates to legitimate legal proceedings
- Ineffective assistance of counsel claims on appeal require specificity about what arguments were confusing or what evidence went unintroduced---vague assertions will not succeed

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of Massachusetts. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
