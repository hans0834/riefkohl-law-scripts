# California Appellate Court Conditionally Reverses Adoption Order,
Finding Insufficient Inquiry Under the Indian Child Welfare Act
Regarding Minor's Potential Native American Ancestry

**Court:** Court of Appeal, Third District, California
**Date:** August 11, 2025 --- 2025 WL 2303178

---

## Introduction

E.L.'s mother was arrested in November 2020 for killing the child's
father. The paternal grandparents took custody and obtained guardianship
in December 2020. In December 2024, the grandparents filed a petition to
free E.L. from the mother's custody for adoption. At a January 2025
hearing, the mother disclosed that her half-brother was affiliated with
a Navajo tribe in Arizona and that other relatives possibly had Native
American ancestry. An ICWA-030 form was filed listing possible
eligibility for Navajo, Cherokee, Apache, and a fourth tribe listed in the court filing (**[Editorial Note: No federally recognized tribe by the name "Turanaco" has been identified in federal records. The tribal name referenced in this case summary has not been verified against the original ICWA-030 form and may be inaccurate.]**). The case centered on whether the probate court fulfilled its duty of inquiry under ICWA before proceeding with the adoption.

## The Procedural Background

A court investigator initially stated the mother had no Native American
ancestry based on the paternal grandmother's statement. After the
mother's January 2025 disclosure, ICWA notices were sent to tribes and
the BIA. Six tribes responded negatively; four tribes were unresponsive.
No contact was made with the State Department of Social Services, and no
inquiry with maternal extended family was documented. The probate court
proceeded with the adoption petition. The mother appealed.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

Whether the probate court complied with the Indian Child Welfare Act's
affirmative, continuing duty of inquiry regarding the minor's potential
Native American ancestry before proceeding with the adoption.

## Arguments Presented to the Court

Mother/JN (Appellant): Argued the court failed to comply with ICWA's
mandatory inquiry requirements, including failure to contact maternal
extended family, failure to notify the State Department of Social
Services, and failure to follow up with unresponsive tribes.

Grandparents/CH (Respondents): Argued sufficient inquiry had been
conducted.

## The Court's Holding

Conditionally reversed and remanded.

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

ICWA requires an affirmative, continuing duty to inquire whether a child
is or might be an Indian child. This includes asking extended family
members, contacting the BIA and State Department of Social Services, and
contacting tribes. The record showed incomplete inquiry: no contact with
maternal family was documented, no State Department of Social Services
notification was made, and four tribes remained unresponsive. When
inquiry is incomplete, conditional reversal is required for further
proceedings. If further inquiry shows ICWA applies, the adoption proceedings must
comply with ICWA requirements, including higher evidentiary standards,
placement preferences, and active efforts to prevent the breakup of the
Indian family; if no application is shown, the judgment is to be
reinstated.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision is a critical reminder that ICWA compliance in adoption proceedings is not optional and cannot be shortcut. The court's conditional reversal demonstrates that even when the adoption petition appears meritorious, incomplete inquiry into a child's potential Native American ancestry will result in reversal and remand. Practitioners handling adoption cases must treat ICWA inquiry as an affirmative, continuing obligation that requires documented outreach to all potential sources of ancestry information.

Key practice points include:
- ICWA inquiry must include documented contact with maternal and paternal extended family members, the BIA, the State Department of Social Services, and all potentially relevant tribes---relying solely on one family member's statement is insufficient
- When tribes are unresponsive to ICWA notices, practitioners must follow up rather than treating silence as a negative response; unresponsive tribes create an incomplete record that supports reversal
- Conditional reversal is the standard remedy for incomplete ICWA inquiry: if further inquiry reveals ICWA applicability, the adoption proceedings must comply with ICWA requirements (higher evidentiary standards, placement preferences, and active efforts); if not, the original judgment is reinstated

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of California. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
