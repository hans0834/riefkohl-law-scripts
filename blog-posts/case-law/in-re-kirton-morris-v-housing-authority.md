# New York Appellate Division Affirms Stay of Estate Turnover Proceeding
Pending Resolution of Quiet Title Action Involving Decedent's Brooklyn
Property

**Court:** Supreme Court, Appellate Division, Second Department, New York
**Date:** March 19, 2025 --- 2025 WL 850203 (Argued June 18, 2024)

---

## Introduction

Germaine Kirton died in 1990. In 2014, Housing Urban Development LLC
commenced a quiet title action regarding real property in Brooklyn
previously owned by the decedent and her predeceased husband. In early
2020, the Surrogate's Court appointed Brian Morris (the decedent's
nephew) as temporary administrator solely to represent the estate's
interests in the quiet title action. Later in 2020, the court issued
full letters of administration to Morris. The key question on appeal was whether the Surrogate's Court properly exercised its discretion in staying the turnover proceeding while the quiet title action was pending.

## The Procedural Background

Morris, as administrator, petitioned under SCPA 2103 for turnover of
certain property and funds on behalf of the estate. The Surrogate's
Court (Judge Bernard J. Graham) stayed the SCPA 2103 proceeding pending
resolution of the quiet title action (Housing Urban Dev., LLC v. Morris,
Supreme Court, Kings County, Index No. 502449/14). Morris appealed. The appellate court treated the notice of appeal as an application for leave to appeal and granted leave.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

Whether the Surrogate's Court properly stayed the estate turnover
proceeding pending resolution of the quiet title action.

## Arguments Presented to the Court

Morris (Appellant): Challenged the stay, raising arguments about
errors in a related SCPA 2113 proceeding to determine distributees'
rights.

Housing Urban Development LLC (Respondent): Supported the stay
pending resolution of the quiet title action that would determine
property ownership.

## The Court's Holding

Affirmed, with costs.

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

The court found that Morris's arguments on appeal related to alleged
errors in a separate SCPA 2113 proceeding to determine distributees'
rights---issues that were not determined in the order being appealed.
Those contentions were therefore not properly before the appellate
court.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision highlights two important lessons for estate administrators. First, when a quiet title action is pending over estate property, a Surrogate's Court may properly stay related SCPA 2103 turnover proceedings until property ownership is resolved. Second, appellate arguments must address the specific order being appealed---raising issues from a separate SCPA 2113 proceeding on appeal of a stay order will be rejected as not properly before the court.

Key practice points include:
- Estate administrators should expect that turnover proceedings under SCPA 2103 may be stayed when a related quiet title action involving the same property is pending in another court
- On appeal, practitioners must limit their arguments to issues actually decided in the order under review; arguments relating to separate proceedings (such as an SCPA 2113 distributees' rights determination) will be dismissed as not properly before the appellate court
- When a Surrogate's Court grants letters of administration decades after death for the limited purpose of defending estate interests in litigation, the administrator's authority and obligations are narrowly defined by that purpose

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of New York. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
