# Mississippi Court of Appeals Affirms Dismissal of Asbestos Wrongful
Death Claim for Lack of Prosecution After Nine-Year Period of Inactivity

**Court:** Court of Appeals of Mississippi (Appeal from Jones County Circuit Court,
Second Judicial District, Hon. Dal Williamson, Judge)
**Date:** August 12, 2025 --- 2025 WL 2317904, No. 2023-CA-01196-COA

---

## Introduction

Salvador Parra filed suit in 2009 against 19 companies for harm from
asbestos exposure. He claimed exposure while employed in Mississippi in
1968--1969 at jobsites in Hattiesburg, Clarksdale, Sandersville, Sardis,
and Tylertown. The defendants allegedly mined, designed, manufactured,
and sold industrial products containing asbestos. Parra was a Louisiana
citizen. He died in 2011, and his wife Peggy was substituted as
plaintiff representative of his estate. The case raised the question of whether dismissal for failure to prosecute was appropriate given the lengthy dormancy period and whether lesser sanctions should have been considered first.

## The Procedural Background

The suit was filed in 2009; discovery was exchanged in 2009--2011. After
Salvador's death in 2011, the case became dormant with no substantive
activity for over nine years (November 2011 to February 2021). In
January 2021, defendants Shell Oil and Bechtel filed motions to dismiss
for lack of prosecution. The trial court dismissed the entire case with
prejudice in June 2021. Peggy Parra appealed.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

Whether the trial court properly dismissed the case for failure to
prosecute given the nine-year dormancy period, and whether lesser
sanctions should have been considered.

## Arguments Presented to the Court

Estate (Appellant): Argued that inactivity was partly due to one
defendant (Marsh) being subject to a federal bankruptcy court
injunction, and that lesser sanctions should have been imposed before
dismissal.

Defendants (Appellees): Argued the nine-year delay prejudiced their
ability to defend and that plaintiff was free to pursue other defendants
not subject to the bankruptcy injunction.

## The Court's Holding

Affirmed. The dismissal for lack of prosecution was within the trial
court's discretion.

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

Under Mississippi law, a court may dismiss for failure to prosecute
where there is a clear record of delay or contumacious conduct. The
plaintiff bears the obligation to pursue the case with diligence.
Despite one defendant being subject to a bankruptcy injunction, the
plaintiff was free to pursue the remaining 18 defendants. The extended
delay prejudiced defendants' ability to defend. The court found that
lesser sanctions were not appropriate given the 16-year case timeframe,
more than nine years of complete inactivity, and procedural history with
more docket entries for cancellations than depositions.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision demonstrates that courts will not tolerate extended periods of inactivity, even when partial justification exists. The plaintiff's argument that one defendant's bankruptcy injunction excused the nine-year dormancy was rejected because the plaintiff remained free to pursue the other 18 defendants. The court also found that lesser sanctions were inappropriate given the extreme length of the delay and the prejudice to defendants' ability to mount a defense.

Key practice points include:
- A bankruptcy stay affecting one defendant does not excuse failure to prosecute claims against remaining co-defendants; practitioners must actively pursue available claims or risk dismissal of the entire case
- In Mississippi, courts evaluating dismissal for failure to prosecute consider the overall case timeframe, length of inactivity, prejudice to defendants, and the docket record---a history showing more cancellations than depositions weighs heavily against the plaintiff
- When a party dies during litigation, successor counsel or the estate representative must promptly resume prosecution; a substitution of parties alone does not excuse subsequent years of inactivity

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of Mississippi. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
