# Massachusetts Appeals Court Affirms Contempt Finding Against Former Wife
for Failure to Pay Child Support and Return Child to Custodial Father

**Court:** Appeals Court of Massachusetts
**Date:** August 11, 2025 --- 2025 WL 2304430

---

## Introduction

The parties divorced in 2012, with the wife initially having sole
custody. A June 2017 stipulation gave the husband sole custody; the wife
was not required to pay support if unemployed but had to notify within
48 hours of gaining employment. The husband counterclaimed for child
support after the wife found employment. A temporary order set \$38/week
support. In January 2021, the parties stipulated that the wife would
return the child and continue \$38/week support. The wife failed to
return the child or pay support. A \$4,636 arrearage accumulated. The case raised the questions of whether the wife had the present ability to pay the ordered child support and whether retroactive modification of the support obligation was appropriate.

## The Procedural Background

In July 2018, the wife filed a modification complaint seeking custody;
the husband counterclaimed for support. In February 2021, the husband
filed a contempt complaint. In April 2021, the wife was found in
contempt and assessed a \$10 daily fine. In June 2023, the husband filed
a second contempt complaint. In September 2023, the judge held a
hearing, found the wife in contempt, and ordered \$38 weekly payment
toward the arrearage. The wife appealed.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

(1) Whether the wife had the present ability to pay child support; and
(2) whether retroactive modification of the support obligation was
appropriate.

## Arguments Presented to the Court

Wife (Appellant): Argued she lacked present ability to pay and that
retroactive modification should excuse her arrears.

Husband (Appellee): Argued the wife had the ability to pay
(financial statement showed \$700 weekly income and substantial home
equity) and failed to comply with court orders.

## The Court's Holding

Affirmed.

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

The wife failed to meet her burden of proving present inability to pay.
The judge credited a prior \$342 payment and considered the totality of
circumstances, including her financial statement showing \$700 weekly
income and substantial home equity. On retroactive modification, the
statute prohibits retroactive relief except from the date of service of
a pending modification complaint. The wife's self-help in taking custody
without court authorization made retroactive remedy unjust.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision reinforces two critical principles in family law enforcement. First, a party seeking to avoid a contempt finding for failure to pay child support bears the burden of proving present inability to pay, and financial evidence showing income and assets (such as home equity) will undermine that defense. Second, a party who takes physical custody of a child without court authorization cannot later seek retroactive modification of support obligations to offset the arrears accumulated during the unauthorized custody period.

Key practice points include:
- In contempt proceedings for failure to pay child support, the obligor bears the burden of proving present inability to pay; financial statements showing income and substantial assets will defeat this defense
- Retroactive modification of child support is generally prohibited except from the date of service of a pending modification complaint, preventing parties from using self-help custody changes to create retroactive credit
- Practitioners representing parties in custody disputes must advise clients that taking physical custody without court authorization creates both a contempt risk and a bar to retroactive support modification

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of Massachusetts. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
