This California appellate decision affirms that specific performance is generally available for real property contracts involving trust assets, even over a successor trustee's objections. The ruling provides important guidance on conditions precedent in trust property sales and the consequences of failing to timely raise procedural objections.

# California Appellate Court Affirms Specific Performance Judgment
Ordering Trust to Transfer Real Property Pursuant to Purchase Agreement,
Rejecting Successor Trustee's Challenges to Conditions Precedent

**Court:** Court of Appeal, Second District, California
**Date:** March 19, 2025 --- 2025 WL 852659

---

## Introduction

Julius Burton Sr. created the Julius and Christopher Burton Family Trust
in 1998 holding real property including Crenshaw properties. After
Julius Sr.'s death in 2006, Regions Bank became successor trustee with
power to sell trust property. In April 2014, Regions and Shabani entered
an amended purchase agreement for the property at \$685,000. However,
litigation between Regions and Julius Jr. (the trust beneficiary)
delayed the sale. In November 2016, Regions and Julius Jr. settled.
Shabani sought specific performance of the purchase agreement. The case raised questions about whether the trial court should have deferred to a related probate proceeding, whether conditions precedent to the sale were satisfied, and whether specific performance was an appropriate remedy.

## The Procedural Background

Shabani filed a motion for summary judgment in May 2018 on his breach of
contract claim. The trial court granted summary judgment for Shabani in
July 2019. In June 2023, Shabani elected specific performance as his
remedy, and the trial court granted judgment directing title transfer
with \$685,000 deposited into escrow. Cal Burton, the successor trustee, appealed.

## The Legal Issue at Stake

(1) Whether the trial court should have awaited a probate court ruling
on related case notice before ruling on summary judgment; (2) whether
triable issues existed regarding conditions precedent to the purchase
agreement (trust committee approval, probate court approval); and (3)
whether Shabani was entitled to specific performance.

## Arguments Presented to the Court

Burton (Appellant): Argued the trial court should not have ruled
without probate court guidance, that triable issues existed regarding
trust committee approval and other conditions precedent, and that
Shabani failed to prove damages.

Shabani (Respondent): Argued there were no triable issues because
all conditions precedent were satisfied, Regions had represented
authorization to proceed, and the probate court had ratified and
approved Regions' acts.

## The Court's Holding

Affirmed. Judgment for specific performance upheld.

## Detailed Analysis and Reasoning

Burton forfeited the related-case issue by failing to raise it timely.
On conditions precedent, the undisputed evidence showed all
contingencies were removed: both parties executed contingency removal
documents, Regions represented authorization to proceed, and the probate
court found Regions' acts ratified, confirmed, and approved. On trust
committee approval, Regions had represented to both Shabani and the
probate court that the committee approved the sale. Regarding specific
performance, the court held it is generally available for real property
contracts absent equitable reasons for denial, and a party seeking
enforcement need not prove damages are inadequate.

## Practical Significance for Legal Practitioners

This decision provides important guidance for trust property transactions and successor trustee obligations. The court confirmed that specific performance is generally available as a remedy for real property purchase agreements involving trust assets, and that a buyer need not separately prove damages are inadequate. Critically, the ruling demonstrates that a successor trustee inherits the binding commitments made by a predecessor trustee, and that probate court ratification of a trustee's acts can foreclose later challenges to transaction authorization.

Key practice points include:
- When a trustee executes a purchase agreement for trust real property, successor trustees are bound by that commitment; specific performance may be ordered even over a successor trustee's objections about conditions precedent
- Failure to timely raise procedural objections (such as related-case notice requirements) results in forfeiture of those arguments on appeal---trustees must raise all objections at the trial court level
- Execution of contingency removal documents and representations of authorization by a trustee create strong evidence that conditions precedent have been satisfied, particularly when corroborated by probate court ratification of the trustee's acts

## How This Applies in Puerto Rico

**Important Jurisdictional Note:** This case was decided under the common law of California. Puerto Rico is a civil-law jurisdiction with a fundamentally different legal framework. Puerto Rico trusts are governed by Law 219-2012, and succession is governed by the Civil Code (Ley 55-2020), which includes forced heirship rules that have no equivalent in most U.S. states. The holding in this case does not apply as precedent in Puerto Rico courts.
