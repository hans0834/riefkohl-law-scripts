# Puerto Rico Trust Law Case Summaries

Navigating trust litigation in Puerto Rico requires understanding who has standing to bring claims, what procedural rights beneficiaries enjoy, and how trust liquidation works under local law. These case summaries examine key appellate decisions that define the procedural framework for trust disputes, including questions of party standing, intervention, and trust wind-down procedures.

## 1. RAS Caribbean Industrial Manufacturing Corporation v. Fideicomiso De Conservación De Puerto Rico

**Headline:** Trust can be sued directly without individual service of process on all fiduciaries.

**Court:** Tribunal de Apelaciones de Puerto Rico (Puerto Rico Court of Appeals)

**Date:** 2009

**Summary of Relevant Facts:** RAS Caribbean Industrial Manufacturing Corporation initiated legal proceedings against Fideicomiso De Conservación De Puerto Rico (Puerto Rico Conservation Trust). The case involved questions about proper service of process requirements for trust entities and whether individual fiduciaries must be personally served with process.

**Procedural Background:** The case was brought before the Tribunal de Apelaciones after lower court proceedings. The central procedural issue involved whether service of process had been properly accomplished on the trust entity.

**Main Controversies:** Whether a trust can be sued as an entity without serving process on each individual fiduciary; the proper procedure for service of process on trusts under Puerto Rico law; whether the trust's juridical personality allows it to be named and served as a party.

**Position of the Parties:** RAS Caribbean argued that it had properly served the trust entity. The trust (through its fiduciaries) contested the adequacy of service, claiming that individual fiduciaries must be separately served with process.

**Holding/Decision:** The court held that a trust can be sued directly and that service of process on the trust entity is valid without requiring individual service on each fiduciary.

**Reasons for the Decision:** Trusts have juridical personality under Puerto Rico law and can be party to litigation. The court recognized that requiring service of process on each individual fiduciary would create practical impediments to justice and would treat trusts differently from other legal entities. Service on the trust entity through its fiduciaries or designated agents is legally sufficient.

## 3. Benitez-Bithorn v. Rossello-Gonzalez

**Headline:** Trust has standing to sue in federal court and removal jurisdiction protections apply to certain trust fiduciaries under federal law.

**Court:** United States District Court for the District of Puerto Rico

**Date:** 2002

**Summary of Relevant Facts:** A trust entity (through Benitez-Bithorn) initiated litigation against Rossello-Gonzalez. The case involved questions about whether a trust has independent standing to sue in federal court and whether certain fiduciaries or trust representatives are entitled to removal jurisdiction protections.

**Procedural Background:** The trust brought suit in what may have originally been a state court proceeding, or questions arose regarding whether the case could be removed to federal court. The parties disputed whether the trust had proper federal court standing and whether 28 U.S.C. § 1442(a)(2) removal protections applied.

**Main Controversies:** Whether a trust has independent standing to sue in federal court; whether trust fiduciaries acting in their official capacity can remove cases under 28 U.S.C. § 1442(a)(2); the nature of trust juridical personality for federal jurisdiction purposes.

**Position of the Parties:** Benitez-Bithorn/the trust argued it had standing to sue and that removal protections applied. Rossello-Gonzalez opposed the trust's standing or challenged the validity of removal.

**Holding/Decision:** The court recognized that the trust has standing to sue in federal court and addressed the applicability of removal jurisdiction protections under 28 U.S.C. § 1442(a)(2) to trust fiduciaries acting in their official capacities.

**Reasons for the Decision:** The court acknowledged the trust's juridical personality and capacity to sue as an independent legal entity. The court analyzed the federal removal statute and determined that trust fiduciaries acting in their official capacity could qualify for removal protections in appropriate circumstances.
