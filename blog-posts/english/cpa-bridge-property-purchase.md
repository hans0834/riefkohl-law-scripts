## Property Purchase in Puerto Rico: Legal Due Diligence for Act 60 Relocatees

**Keywords:** buying property Puerto Rico Act 60, Puerto Rico real estate attorney

Buying property in Puerto Rico is one of the first things most Act 60 relocatees do — and one of the most legally consequential. Property ownership is a cornerstone of your residency documentation, a significant financial investment, and a transaction governed by a legal system that works differently from what you are accustomed to on the mainland.

Your CPA may have told you that owning property strengthens your residency position for Act 60 purposes. That is correct. But the purchase process itself is a legal matter, and the differences between Puerto Rico real estate law and mainland practice can create expensive surprises for unprepared buyers.

---

### Why Property Ownership Matters for Act 60 Residency

Act 60 benefits depend on establishing bona fide Puerto Rico residency. The IRS evaluates residency using multiple factors, and property ownership is one of the strongest indicators of genuine domicile.

**How property supports your residency claim:**

- Owning (not just renting) a primary residence in Puerto Rico demonstrates commitment and intent to remain
- It is a tangible, verifiable connection that the IRS cannot easily dismiss
- Property tax records, utility bills, and maintenance records create a documentary trail of presence
- Selling or abandoning your mainland residence and purchasing in Puerto Rico strengthens the "closer connection" test

**Important caveat:** Property ownership alone does not establish residency. You still must meet the presence test (183 days), the tax home test, and the closer connection test. But failing to own property — or maintaining a more substantial property on the mainland — is a red flag that the IRS will notice.

### The Puerto Rico Real Estate Transaction: How It Differs

If you have bought property on the mainland, set aside those assumptions. Puerto Rico real estate transactions follow civil law procedures with several critical differences.

#### The Notarial Deed Requirement

All real property transfers in Puerto Rico must be executed by notarial deed (escritura publica) before a Puerto Rico notary public. Unlike on the mainland, where a notary simply witnesses signatures, a Puerto Rico notary is a licensed attorney who drafts the deed, verifies the legal capacity of the parties, and ensures the transaction complies with Puerto Rico law.

The notary is not just a formality. The notary has legal responsibility for the validity of the deed and bears professional liability if the document is defective. Choosing the right notary — one who understands Act 60 issues and real property law — matters.

#### The Property Registry System

Puerto Rico uses a property registry system (Registro de la Propiedad) rather than the recording system common on the mainland. Property rights are established by registration, and the registry records are the primary source of information about ownership, liens, encumbrances, and restrictions.

**What this means for you:** A title search in Puerto Rico involves examining the registry records, not just searching a county recorder's database. The registrar reviews documents for legal sufficiency before recording them, which provides an additional layer of protection but also means that defective documents can be rejected.

#### Title Insurance vs. Legal Review

Title insurance is available in Puerto Rico but is not as universally used as on the mainland. Many transactions rely primarily on the attorney's review of registry records and the notary's certification of clear title. Whether you should obtain title insurance depends on the specific transaction, the property's history, and your risk tolerance.

For Act 60 relocatees making a significant property investment, title insurance provides an additional layer of protection and is generally recommended. But it does not replace the need for a thorough legal review of the registry records.

### Legal Due Diligence Steps

Before you commit to a property purchase in Puerto Rico, your attorney should complete the following:

#### Title Search and Registry Review

A comprehensive review of the property's registry history, including:

- Current ownership and chain of title
- Outstanding mortgages, liens, and encumbrances
- Easements and rights of way
- Restrictive covenants and deed restrictions
- Pending litigation or claims against the property
- Any annotations or notations that could affect title

#### Property Tax Verification (CRIM)

The Centro de Recaudacion de Ingresos Municipales (CRIM) administers property taxes in Puerto Rico. Delinquent property taxes are a lien on the property that survives transfer. Your attorney must verify that all property taxes are current before closing.

**Common issue:** Properties in Puerto Rico sometimes have years of unpaid property taxes. The seller may not disclose this, and the buyer inherits the obligation. A CRIM certification confirming no outstanding taxes should be a condition of closing.

#### Zoning and Land Use Verification

Puerto Rico's planning and zoning laws are administered by the Oficina de Gerencia de Permisos (OGPe) and the Junta de Planificacion. Your attorney should verify that the property's current use and any planned modifications comply with applicable zoning regulations.

#### Environmental Review

Depending on the property's location and history, environmental due diligence may be warranted. Coastal properties, former industrial sites, and properties near wetlands may have environmental restrictions or contamination issues.

#### Condominium Review

If you are purchasing a condominium, additional due diligence is required:

- Review of the condominium master deed and bylaws
- Financial statements of the community of owners (consejo de titulares)
- Outstanding assessments or special assessments
- Pending litigation involving the condominium association
- Insurance coverage for common areas
- Reserve fund adequacy

#### Survey and Boundary Verification

Property boundaries in Puerto Rico should be verified by a licensed surveyor (agrimensor). Boundary disputes are not uncommon, particularly for older properties where historical descriptions may be imprecise.

### Property Tax Considerations

Puerto Rico property taxes are generally lower than mainland rates, but they have their own complexities.

**Assessment system:** Properties are assessed by CRIM, and assessed values are often significantly below market value. Tax rates vary by municipality.

**Exemptions:** Primary residences may qualify for a homestead exemption that reduces the taxable value. Your attorney can determine whether your property qualifies and help you apply.

**Municipal taxes:** In addition to property taxes, some municipalities impose additional assessments or fees. These vary by location and should be factored into your cost analysis.

**Act 60 interaction:** Property tax obligations are separate from your Act 60 decree benefits. Your decree does not exempt you from property taxes.

### Holding Property in a Trust vs. Personally

One of the most important decisions is how to hold title to your Puerto Rico property. The options have different implications for estate planning, asset protection, and Act 60 compliance.

#### Holding Property Personally

**Advantages:**
- Simplest approach with lowest transaction costs
- Property qualifies for homestead exemption if used as primary residence
- Straightforward for residency documentation purposes
- Step-up in basis at death under current federal tax law

**Disadvantages:**
- Property is included in your taxable estate for federal estate tax purposes
- Subject to Puerto Rico probate at your death
- Exposed to personal creditor claims and lawsuits
- Forced heirship rules apply directly

#### Holding Property in a Trust

**Advantages:**
- Can remove property from your taxable estate (if irrevocable trust)
- Avoids Puerto Rico probate at your death
- Provides asset protection from personal creditors
- Allows controlled distribution to heirs, potentially satisfying forced heirship on your terms
- Privacy — trust ownership is less publicly visible than personal ownership

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher initial setup costs
- May not qualify for homestead exemption (depending on trust structure)
- Transfer to trust requires a notarial deed and registry filing
- Must be carefully structured to avoid unintended Act 60 consequences
- Ongoing trust administration costs

#### Holding Property in an LLC or Corporation

Some buyers consider holding property through a Puerto Rico LLC or corporation. This can provide liability protection but introduces additional tax and legal complexity. The entity must be properly formed, maintained, and reported. Your attorney and CPA should evaluate whether entity ownership makes sense for your specific situation.

**The recommendation for most Act 60 relocatees:** Hold your primary residence personally (for residency documentation and homestead exemption purposes) and consider trust or entity ownership for investment properties or as part of your broader estate plan.

### Common Pitfalls for Mainland Buyers

Act 60 relocatees who approach Puerto Rico real estate with mainland assumptions frequently encounter these problems:

**Assuming a title company runs the closing.** On the mainland, a title company or escrow agent typically coordinates the closing. In Puerto Rico, the notary (who is an attorney) drives the transaction. If you do not have your own attorney reviewing the deal, you may be relying entirely on the notary selected by the seller or the real estate agent — who may not be looking out for your interests.

**Skipping the CRIM verification.** Delinquent property taxes are the buyer's problem after closing. Do not assume the seller has paid them.

**Underestimating the closing timeline.** Puerto Rico real estate transactions often take longer than mainland transactions. Registry processing, document preparation, and government certifications can extend timelines. Build in buffer time, especially if your residency timeline depends on the purchase.

**Ignoring flood zone and hurricane exposure.** Puerto Rico is in a hurricane zone. Flood zone determinations, structural assessments, and insurance availability should all be evaluated before purchase. FEMA flood maps may not reflect current risk levels.

**Not considering the resale market.** Act 60 has increased demand for certain types of properties in certain areas, but the Puerto Rico real estate market is not uniform. Your attorney can help you understand the legal and practical considerations that affect long-term value.

**Failing to coordinate with estate planning.** The way you take title at purchase sets the foundation for how the property is treated in your estate. Changing title later requires additional legal work, transfer taxes, and registry filings. It is better to get the ownership structure right at the outset.

### The Role of Your Attorney in the Transaction

Your attorney's involvement in a Puerto Rico property purchase should include:

- Reviewing the purchase agreement before you sign
- Conducting or supervising the title search
- Verifying CRIM tax status and other government certifications
- Reviewing condominium documents if applicable
- Advising on ownership structure (personal, trust, entity)
- Coordinating with your estate planning and Act 60 compliance
- Attending or supervising the closing
- Ensuring proper registry filing after closing
- Advising on post-closing obligations (property tax filings, insurance, etc.)

This is not a role that a real estate agent can fill. Your agent helps you find the right property. Your attorney ensures the transaction protects your interests and integrates with your broader legal and tax plan.
