## Email 2: The Biggest Misconception
**Send:** 3 days after Email 1
**Subject line:** The mainland trust mistake that costs Act 60 investors

---

Hi [FIRST_NAME],

When Act 60 decree holders move to Puerto Rico, most assume their existing mainland trust will work the same way here. It usually does not.

Here is why: Puerto Rico is a civil law jurisdiction. Its trust law — Ley 219-2012 — treats a trust as an *autonomous estate* (patrimonio autónomo), a separate legal person. On the mainland, a trust is just a fiduciary relationship. That difference has real consequences for:

- **Asset protection.** PR trust property belongs to the trust entity, not to you. Creditors of the trustor, trustee, or beneficiary generally cannot reach it.
- **Tax treatment.** The trust's independent legal personality affects how it is taxed at both the PR and federal level.
- **Estate planning.** Puerto Rico has forced heirship (the *legítima*), which reserves a portion of your estate for certain heirs. No trust or will can override it.

The bottom line: a mainland trust sitting in a Puerto Rico estate plan is a square peg in a round hole. It is not necessarily wrong, but it almost always needs to be reviewed.

## Email 3: Case Law That Matters
**Send:** 3 days after Email 2
**Subject line:** What recent court cases tell us about PR trusts

---

Hi [FIRST_NAME],

Puerto Rico trust law is still relatively young, and the courts are actively shaping how it works in practice. Here are two recent developments worth knowing about:

**1. The trust's independence is real — courts enforce it.** In *Fideicomiso El Puente GNR v. Asociación de Residentes* (2024), the court confirmed that a properly registered trust can sue in its own name. This is not just theory — the autonomous estate concept has teeth.

**2. Family property complicates things.** In *Gil Enseñat v. Marini Román* (2009), the court had to untangle a divorcing spouse's interest in a trust funded with community property (*bienes gananciales*). The theoretical clarity of trust autonomy gets messy when marital property is involved.

These cases are not academic. They affect how trusts should be drafted, funded, and administered in Puerto Rico today.

## Email 4: The Practical Stuff
**Send:** 3 days after Email 3
**Subject line:** What a Puerto Rico trust actually costs (and takes)

---

Hi [FIRST_NAME],

One of the most common questions I get: "What does it cost to set up a trust in Puerto Rico?"

Here is a straightforward answer:

**Creating a trust** involves attorney fees for drafting, notary fees for the public deed (*escritura pública*), and registration fees with ODIN (the Special Trust Registry). All inter vivos trusts in Puerto Rico must be executed before a notary and registered with ODIN. The notary must file the required notification within the first ten days of the month following execution.

**Administering a trust** has ongoing costs: trustee fees (if using a professional trustee), annual tax filings, and periodic reviews when circumstances change.

**What most people underestimate** is the cost of *not* doing it right. A trust that is improperly structured for Puerto Rico — or a mainland trust that has not been reviewed for PR law — can create tax exposure, lose asset protection benefits, or run afoul of the forced heirship rules.
