### Introduction (Half page)

## Act 38-2026 Extended Act 60 to 2055 — What This Means for Your Estate Plan

Act 38-2026 extended the availability of Puerto Rico's Act 60 tax incentives through 2055. For existing and future decree holders, this is significant news: the tax benefits that brought you to Puerto Rico now have a planning horizon measured in decades, not years.

But a longer time horizon changes the estate planning calculus. Strategies designed for a 10- or 15-year window need to be rethought for a 30-year window. Multi-generational planning becomes relevant in ways it was not before. And the interaction between Act 60 benefits, federal estate tax rules, and Puerto Rico's forced heirship provisions becomes more consequential the longer the benefits are in effect.

This checklist covers the seven estate planning steps every Act 60 holder should take in response to the 2055 extension.

---

### Step 1 (1/2 page)

## 1. Review Your Existing Trust Structure for a 30+ Year Planning Horizon

If you created a Puerto Rico trust — or brought a mainland trust to PR — when you first relocated, that trust was likely designed for the planning horizon that existed at the time. With Act 60 now extended to 2055, your trust may need to be restructured.

A trust designed for a 10-year window may not address the distribution needs of your heirs over a 30-year period. Trustee succession becomes more important when the trust needs to operate for decades. Investment management provisions, distribution standards, and amendment powers should all be reviewed with the longer time frame in mind.

**What to do:** Have your attorney review your existing trust instrument against a 30-year planning horizon. If your trust was created under Law 219-2012, confirm that its terms are flexible enough to accommodate changes in your family structure, financial situation, and the law itself over the coming decades.

---

### Step 2 (1/2 page)

## 2. Evaluate Multi-Generational Trust Options Under Law 219-2012

The 2055 extension makes multi-generational trust planning viable in a way it was not before. If your children or grandchildren may also qualify for Act 60 decrees, a trust that spans generations can compound the tax advantages over a much longer period.

Law 219-2012 permits trusts with long durations and flexible structures. A dynasty-style trust created under PR law can hold assets for the benefit of multiple generations while maintaining the asset protection benefits of an autonomous estate. The key is ensuring the trust instrument is drafted to account for future beneficiaries, changing tax laws, and the possibility that future generations may or may not reside in Puerto Rico.

**What to do:** Discuss multi-generational trust structures with your attorney. Evaluate whether your current trust should be supplemented with a second trust designed specifically for long-term, multi-generational wealth transfer.

---

### Step 3 (1/2 page)

## 3. Update Forced Heirship Analysis Under Civil Code 2020

Puerto Rico's forced heirship rules were revised in the 2020 Civil Code update. If your estate plan was created or last reviewed before 2020, your forced heirship analysis may be based on outdated rules.

With a 30-year planning horizon, the composition of your forced heirs is likely to change. Children may be born or adopted. Marriages may begin or end. The *legitima* calculation must be revisited as your family structure evolves. A forced heirship analysis that was accurate five years ago may not reflect your current situation — and the longer your Act 60 benefits remain in effect, the larger the estate that will be subject to these rules.

**What to do:** Have your attorney conduct a fresh forced heirship analysis under the current Civil Code. If your family structure has changed since your last review, update your estate plan to reflect the current class of forced heirs and the current *legitima* calculation.

---

### Step 4 (1/2 page)

## 4. Coordinate Federal Estate Tax Planning with the Permanent $15M Exemption

The federal estate tax exemption was made permanent at $15 million per individual by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 2025), with annual inflation indexing. This removes the uncertainty that previously complicated long-term planning.

For Act 60 holders, the permanent exemption creates an opportunity: with a known exemption amount and a 30-year horizon for Act 60 benefits, you can implement lifetime gifting strategies and irrevocable trust funding with confidence that the exemption will not revert. Coordinating federal estate tax planning with your Act 60 strategy — and with Puerto Rico's separate estate tax — requires both your CPA and your attorney working together.

**What to do:** Review your federal estate tax exposure with your CPA and attorney. Evaluate whether lifetime gifting, irrevocable trust funding, or other exemption utilization strategies make sense given the permanent $15M exemption and the extended Act 60 horizon.

---

### Step 5 (1/2 page)

## 5. Review Decree Succession Implications

The 2055 extension means Act 60 benefits will be available for decades — but your decree is still personal to you. It does not transfer to your heirs at death. With a longer benefit period, the value of those benefits over the remaining term of your decree is potentially much greater, making succession planning more important than ever.

If your heirs are eligible for their own decrees, the planning involves ensuring a smooth transition. If they are not eligible, the planning involves structuring your estate to minimize the tax impact of losing decree benefits. Either way, the 2055 extension increases the stakes.

**What to do:** Work with your attorney to evaluate decree succession options. Determine whether your heirs meet the eligibility requirements for their own Act 60 decrees, and structure your estate plan to account for the transition — whether it involves decree succession or the loss of benefits.

---

### Step 6 (1/2 page)

## 6. Update Domicile Documentation for Long-Term Residency

If you relocated recently, your domicile documentation may focus on establishing initial residency. But with a 30-year planning horizon, you need documentation that demonstrates ongoing, long-term domicile in Puerto Rico — not just the move, but the permanent commitment.

The IRS can audit Act 60 compliance years after the fact. Maintaining a contemporaneous record of your domicile — updated regularly — is the best defense against a residency challenge. The longer you hold the decree, the more important it is that your documentation demonstrates continuous, bona fide residency.

**What to do:** Work with your attorney to create or update a domicile binder that documents your PR residency on an ongoing basis. Establish an annual review process to ensure your domicile evidence is current and comprehensive. Include voter registration, property records, banking relationships, social ties, and a written statement of intent to remain.

---

### Step 7 (1/2 page)

## 7. Schedule Annual Estate Plan Reviews to Account for Legislative Changes

Act 38-2026 is a reminder that the legal and tax landscape in Puerto Rico is not static. The laws that govern your estate plan — Law 219-2012, the Civil Code, the PR tax code, and federal tax law — are all subject to change. A 30-year planning horizon virtually guarantees that your plan will need to be updated multiple times.

An annual review with your attorney and CPA ensures that your estate plan stays aligned with current law and your evolving circumstances. It also provides an opportunity to address life changes — new family members, asset acquisitions, business developments — before they create planning gaps.

**What to do:** Schedule an annual estate plan and tax strategy review with your attorney and CPA. Use this review to assess whether legislative changes, family changes, or financial changes require updates to your trusts, estate plan, or domicile documentation.
