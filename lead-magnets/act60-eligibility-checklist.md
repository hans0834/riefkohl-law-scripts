### Introduction

## Before You Apply, Confirm You Qualify

Act 60 offers two paths to Puerto Rico's tax incentives — the individual investor decree (Chapter 2) and the export services decree (Chapter 3). The benefits are significant: 0% tax on qualifying capital gains and dividends, or a 4% corporate rate on export services income.

But Act 60 is also one of the most heavily-scrutinized federal tax compliance areas in the United States. The IRS has audited residency claims years after the fact, and decree revocations are increasingly common when applicants fail to meet the eligibility tests on paper or in practice.

This checklist walks through the key eligibility requirements before you commit to an application. Work through each section honestly. If you check most of the boxes, an application is likely worth pursuing. If you cannot check the boxes in any one section, that section is where your application will fail.

---

### Section 1: Threshold Eligibility (Apply to Both Chapters)

## Bona Fide Puerto Rico Residency — IRS §937

To claim Act 60 benefits, you must be a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico under federal law (IRC §937). This is a federal test, not a Puerto Rico test, and the IRS applies it strictly.

**☐ Presence test.** Will you be physically present in Puerto Rico for at least 183 days during the tax year?

**☐ Tax home test.** Will Puerto Rico be your tax home — the location of your regular or principal place of business — during the tax year?

**☐ Closer connection test.** Will your closer connection be to Puerto Rico than to any other country, considering factors like family location, primary residence, banking, voter registration, and personal belongings?

**☐ Six-year non-residency window (post-2026 applications).** For applications filed after 2026, were you NOT a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico for the six tax years immediately preceding your move? Recent applicants who lived in Puerto Rico in the prior six years are excluded from the new individual investor decrees.

If you cannot check all four boxes, the IRS bona fide residency test is the wall your application will hit. We can sometimes structure timing and documentation to meet the test, but a candid review now saves significant cost later.

### Section 2: Chapter 2 — Individual Investor Decree

## Are You Pursuing the Capital Gains and Dividend Exemption?

The Chapter 2 individual investor decree provides 0% tax on qualifying capital gains and dividends earned after you become a Puerto Rico resident. It is the path most relocators pursue.

**☐ Capital gains source.** Will the capital gains you intend to shelter be earned on assets acquired after you become a Puerto Rico resident? Pre-move appreciation generally remains subject to US federal tax — only post-move appreciation qualifies for the 0% rate.

**☐ Dividend source.** Will the dividends you intend to shelter be paid by Puerto Rico-sourced corporations or by entities structured to satisfy the Puerto Rico-source rules?

**☐ Annual donation.** Are you prepared to make the required annual donation ($10,000 per year, allocated 50% to a list of approved nonprofits and 50% to organizations addressing child poverty in Puerto Rico)?

**☐ Real estate purchase.** Are you prepared to acquire a Puerto Rico residential property within two years of receiving your decree, and to use it as your primary residence?

**☐ Annual filing.** Are you prepared to file an annual report with the Department of Economic Development (DDEC) confirming your continued compliance with the decree's terms?

If you cannot check all five boxes, Chapter 2 is not the right path — or the path requires significant restructuring before you apply.

---

### Section 3: Chapter 3 — Export Services Decree

## Are You Pursuing the 4% Corporate Tax Rate on Service Income?

The Chapter 3 export services decree provides a 4% corporate income tax rate on qualifying export services income. It applies to a Puerto Rico entity, not to you personally.

**☐ Eligible service.** Does your business perform services from Puerto Rico for clients located outside Puerto Rico — and do those services fall within the categories listed in Act 60 (consulting, software development, financial services, advertising, R&D, and others)?

**☐ Customer location.** Will your customers be located outside Puerto Rico, and will the services be consumed outside Puerto Rico? Services for Puerto Rico customers do not qualify.

**☐ Operating presence.** Are you prepared to establish and maintain a real operating presence in Puerto Rico — office space, employees physically working from Puerto Rico, and the services genuinely performed from the island?

**☐ Substance over form.** Will the work that generates the service income actually be performed in Puerto Rico, by Puerto Rico-based personnel? The IRS has aggressively challenged Chapter 3 structures where the "Puerto Rico" entity is a shell and the work is performed elsewhere.

**☐ Annual report and compliance.** Are you prepared to file annual compliance reports with DDEC and maintain documented evidence of all the requirements above?

If you cannot check all five boxes, Chapter 3 is not the right path — and pursuing it without substance creates significant IRS audit risk.

### Section 4: Risk Factors That Disqualify or Complicate

## Common Issues That Stop Decrees Cold

**☐ Are you a current Puerto Rico resident, or were you a Puerto Rico resident at any time in the past six years?** Post-2026 individual investor applications exclude prior PR residents within a six-year window.

**☐ Do you have unresolved US federal tax issues, audits, or pending litigation?** DDEC and the IRS coordinate on Act 60 applicant scrutiny. Unresolved tax issues generally need to be cleaned up before a decree application.

**☐ Are your capital gains primarily from assets acquired before you become a PR resident?** The 0% capital gains benefit applies to post-move appreciation. Pre-move appreciation generally remains taxable at federal rates and may need to be realized before you move (or sheltered through other planning) to maximize benefit.

**☐ Do you intend to maintain significant personal or business ties to a state with high taxes (California, New York, etc.)?** State residency challenges are a significant risk for Act 60 holders. Some high-tax states have aggressively pursued former residents who claim PR residency.

**☐ Are you relying on the decree alone — without coordinated estate, trust, and corporate planning?** Act 60 benefits work best as part of a comprehensive plan. Without coordinated planning, decree holders often pay more federal tax than necessary or expose the decree to revocation risk.

### Next Steps

## How Many Boxes Did You Check?

**All boxes in Section 1 + all boxes in Section 2 or 3:** Your application is likely viable. The next step is a structured eligibility consultation to confirm and start preparing the application.

**Most boxes checked, but some uncertainty:** Many issues can be addressed through pre-application structuring. A consultation can identify which boxes need attention and how to position the application for approval.

**Multiple uncheckable boxes:** Act 60 may not be the right fit, or a different timeline may make sense. A consultation can confirm whether your situation can be restructured to qualify.
