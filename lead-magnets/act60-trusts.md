### Introduction (Half page)

You moved to Puerto Rico for the tax benefits. But have you considered what happens to your wealth — and your tax savings — after you're gone?

Most Act 60 decree holders focus exclusively on income tax optimization and overlook estate planning entirely. This guide covers the five most important things you need to know about Puerto Rico trusts and how they interact with your Act 60 decree.

---

### #1: Act 60 Benefits Don't Extend to Estate Tax

**The surprise:** Your Act 60 decree exempts you from income taxes on Puerto Rico-sourced capital gains, interest, and dividends. But it does NOT exempt your estate from the federal estate tax or Puerto Rico's estate tax.

**What this means:** An asset that generated tax-free income during your lifetime is still part of your taxable estate at death. If your estate exceeds the federal exemption ($15 million per individual in 2026, made permanent by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act with inflation indexing), your heirs could face a 40% federal estate tax rate.

**The takeaway:** Estate planning is not optional — it's the necessary complement to your Act 60 strategy.

---

### #2: Puerto Rico Has Forced Heirship Rules

**The surprise:** Unlike most mainland states, Puerto Rico law reserves one-half of your estate for your forced heirs — your children and surviving spouse (the *legítima*). You cannot freely dispose of this portion.

**What this means:**
- 1/2 divided equally among forced heirs (children and surviving spouse)
- 1/2 freely disposable (libre disposición)

**The takeaway:** Your mainland will or trust probably doesn't account for these rules. A Puerto Rico attorney must review your estate plan.

---

### #3: Law 219-2012 Created a Modern Trust Framework

**The opportunity:** Before 2012, Puerto Rico lacked a comprehensive trust statute. Law 219-2012 changed that, creating a flexible framework for both revocable and irrevocable trusts.

**Key benefits of Puerto Rico trusts:**
- Avoid the *declaratoria de herederos* (probate equivalent) — saves months/years
- Asset protection from creditors (irrevocable trusts)
- Estate tax reduction by removing assets from your taxable estate
- Incapacity planning without court-appointed guardianship
- Privacy (trusts are not public records)

**The takeaway:** PR trusts are a powerful tool that most Act 60 holders aren't using.

---

### #4: Your Mainland Trust May Not Work Here

**The risk:** A revocable living trust created by a Delaware, Florida, or California attorney was designed for common law states. Puerto Rico is a civil law jurisdiction.

**Potential problems:**
- Trust may not be recognized the same way in PR courts
- Governing law conflicts (your trust says "Delaware law" but your domicile is PR)
- Forced heirship rules can override trust provisions
- Tax treatment may differ under PR tax code

**The takeaway:** Have your existing trust reviewed by a PR-licensed attorney. You may need a new Puerto Rico trust that works alongside your existing structures.

---

### #5: Timing Matters — For Asset Protection and Tax Planning

**For asset protection:** Transferring assets to an irrevocable trust after a lawsuit or creditor claim is filed can be treated as a fraudulent transfer and reversed. The time to plan is when you have no legal problems.

**For estate tax planning:** The federal estate tax exemption was made permanent at $15 million per individual by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 2025), with annual inflation indexing. Using strategies like lifetime gifting and irrevocable trusts to fully utilize your exemption can lock in significant savings.

**For residency defense:** Your executor may need to defend your Act 60 residency status after you're gone. Organized records and proper documentation in your estate plan protect against posthumous IRS challenges.

**The takeaway:** Every year you delay estate planning, you lose potential protection and tax savings.
