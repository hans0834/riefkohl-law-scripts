### Introduction (1 page)

## Why Tax Planning Without Legal Planning Is Incomplete

Your CPA is indispensable. They structured your Act 60 decree application, manage your tax compliance, and optimize your investment strategy for the incentives Puerto Rico offers. None of what follows diminishes that work.

But tax planning operates within a legal framework — and in Puerto Rico, that framework is different from anything on the mainland. Puerto Rico is a civil law jurisdiction. Its inheritance rules, trust laws, property transfer requirements, and domicile standards are rooted in a legal tradition that has more in common with continental Europe than with the common law states you came from.

Your CPA knows this. That is probably why you are reading this guide.

The six areas covered in this guide represent the most common legal needs that arise from an Act 60 tax strategy. Each one falls outside the scope of tax advisory. Each one has consequences if left unaddressed. And each one requires a Puerto Rico-licensed attorney to resolve.

This is not about replacing your CPA. It is about completing the planning that your CPA started.

### Section 1 (1 page)

## Trust Creation and Structuring Under Law 219-2012

Puerto Rico enacted its modern trust statute — Law 219-2012, the Uniform Trust Code of Puerto Rico — to create a flexible legal framework for trusts on the island. Before 2012, Puerto Rico lacked a comprehensive trust law, and trusts operated in a legal gray area.

**What makes PR trusts different:**

Law 219-2012 defines a trust as an *autonomous estate* (patrimonio autonomo) — a separate legal entity. On the mainland, a trust is a fiduciary relationship, not a separate legal person. This distinction has real consequences:

- Trust property in Puerto Rico belongs to the trust itself, not to the trustor, trustee, or beneficiary. This creates a level of asset protection that mainland trusts cannot replicate.
- The trust has its own legal identity and can hold property, enter contracts, and sue or be sued in its own name.
- Puerto Rico trusts are irrevocable by default. The trustor cannot reserve the right to revoke unless the trust instrument specifically permits it. Act 60 decree holders have a statutory exception under Section 45147(b) that allows revocable trusts, but this must be properly structured.

**What your CPA cannot do here:**

Your CPA can advise on the tax treatment of trust income and the tax consequences of funding a trust. But creating the trust — drafting the trust instrument, executing it before a notary (required under PR law), registering it with ODIN (the Special Trust Registry), and ensuring it complies with Law 219-2012 — requires an attorney. If you have an existing mainland trust, an attorney must review it to determine whether it functions as intended under Puerto Rico law.

**What happens if you skip this:**

A mainland trust sitting in a Puerto Rico estate is a common law instrument in a civil law jurisdiction. It may not provide the asset protection you expect. It may create tax complications. And it may not be enforceable in PR courts the way it would be on the mainland.

### Section 2 (1 page)

## Forced Heirship: How Puerto Rico Law Overrides Your Mainland Estate Plan

Forced heirship is the single most consequential difference between Puerto Rico estate law and mainland estate law. Most Act 60 holders do not learn about it until it is too late.

**How it works:**

Under Puerto Rico's Civil Code, one-half of your estate is reserved for your forced heirs — your children (by blood or adoption) and your surviving spouse. This reserved portion is called the *legitima*. The other half is freely disposable.

- 1/2 of your estate: the *legitima*, divided equally among forced heirs
- 1/2 of your estate: freely disposable (*libre disposicion*)

**Why mainland estate plans fail:**

If you created your estate plan in a common law state, it was designed for a legal system where you can leave your assets to anyone you choose. In Puerto Rico, you cannot. Your will, trust, beneficiary designations, and transfer-on-death arrangements are all subject to forced heirship. If your estate plan distributes more than one-half of your estate outside the class of forced heirs, the forced heirs can challenge it.

**The 2020 Civil Code update:**

Puerto Rico's Civil Code was substantially revised in 2020. The forced heirship provisions were updated, including how the *legitima* is calculated and who qualifies as a forced heir. If your estate plan was last reviewed before 2020, it may not reflect the current rules.

**What your CPA cannot do here:**

Forced heirship is a legal doctrine. It is not a tax issue and it is not within the scope of tax advisory. An attorney must analyze your family structure, identify your forced heirs, calculate the *legitima*, and restructure your estate plan to comply — or to use the legal tools available to minimize its impact while staying within the law.

**What happens if you skip this:**

Your forced heirs can petition the court to override your estate plan after your death. Assets you intended for a surviving spouse, a business partner, or a charity may be redirected to heirs you did not plan for. The resulting litigation delays estate settlement and increases costs for everyone involved.

### Section 3 (1 page)

## Domicile Change Documentation: Legal vs. Tax Requirements

Establishing Puerto Rico residency has two dimensions: the tax dimension and the legal dimension. Your CPA handles the tax side. But the legal evidence of domicile is what protects you if the IRS challenges your Act 60 status.

**The tax filing side (your CPA's domain):**

Filing PR tax returns, meeting the presence test, and documenting your Act 60 compliance are tax requirements. Your CPA manages these.

**The legal evidence side (your attorney's domain):**

Bona fide domicile under federal tax law requires demonstrating that Puerto Rico is your permanent home — not just your tax address. The legal factors that establish domicile include:

- Voter registration in Puerto Rico
- Puerto Rico driver's license
- Banking and financial account relationships in PR
- Real property ownership or long-term lease
- Social, civic, and community ties
- Statements of intent to remain permanently
- Where your closest personal and family relationships are centered
- Professional licenses and business registrations

No single factor is determinative. The IRS and the courts look at the totality of the circumstances.

**Why this requires legal planning:**

Documenting these factors — and creating a contemporaneous record that supports your domicile claim — is a legal exercise. An attorney can advise on which factors to strengthen, how to memorialize your intent, and what documentation to maintain in case of an audit. A domicile binder or affidavit prepared by an attorney carries weight that informal records do not.

**What happens if you skip this:**

If the IRS audits your Act 60 status and determines you were not a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico, your Act 60 benefits can be retroactively revoked. That means back taxes on all income that was exempt under the decree, plus penalties and interest. The tax savings from Act 60 are only as strong as the legal evidence supporting your domicile.

### Section 4 (1 page)

## Decree Succession: Your Act 60 Benefits Don't Transfer at Death

This is the planning gap that catches the most Act 60 holders off guard: your decree benefits expire when you do.

**How decree benefits end:**

Your Act 60 decree is a personal grant from the Puerto Rico government. It applies to you as an individual. When you die, the decree's tax benefits — the exemptions on capital gains, interest, and dividends — do not automatically transfer to your spouse, your children, or your trust.

**What this means for your family:**

If your wealth strategy depends on Act 60 tax advantages, your family needs a plan for what happens when those advantages disappear. Assets that generated tax-exempt income during your lifetime become fully taxable at your death. Without decree succession planning, your heirs may face:

- Federal estate tax on the full value of your estate (up to 40% above the exemption)
- Puerto Rico estate tax
- Loss of ongoing income tax benefits on inherited assets
- Potential need to liquidate assets to cover tax obligations

**What decree succession planning involves:**

An attorney can evaluate whether your heirs are eligible to apply for their own Act 60 decrees, structure your trust and estate plan to minimize the tax impact of losing decree benefits, and coordinate with your CPA on the timing and tax consequences of asset transfers. This is legal structuring, not tax filing.

**What happens if you skip this:**

Your heirs inherit assets but not tax benefits. The wealth you built under Act 60 could be substantially diminished by estate taxes and the loss of income tax exemptions. The entire purpose of your Act 60 strategy — building and preserving wealth in a tax-advantaged environment — is undermined without a succession plan.

### Section 5 (1 page)

## Property Purchase Legal Due Diligence

Most Act 60 decree holders purchase real estate in Puerto Rico within their first year. A home purchase supports your domicile claim and often represents a significant portion of your PR-based assets. But PR real estate transactions involve legal requirements that differ from the mainland.

**What is different about PR real estate:**

- **Notarial requirement.** Real property transfers in Puerto Rico must be executed in a public deed (*escritura publica*) before a notary. In PR, notaries are licensed attorneys — not the notary publics you may be familiar with from the mainland.
- **Title search.** PR title searches examine the property's chain of title in the Property Registry (*Registro de la Propiedad*). Title issues — liens, encumbrances, boundary disputes, undisclosed heirs with forced heirship claims — are common, particularly in older properties.
- **Property tax.** Puerto Rico has its own property tax system (CRIM). Outstanding property taxes create liens that transfer with the property.
- **Community property.** If you are married and purchasing property in PR, the community property rules of the Civil Code may apply to the acquisition, affecting ownership and future disposition.

**What your CPA cannot do here:**

Your CPA can advise on the tax implications of a property purchase — depreciation, deductions, capital gains treatment. But the legal due diligence — title search, deed preparation, lien verification, notarial execution, and Property Registry filing — requires an attorney.

**What happens if you skip this:**

Title defects discovered after closing can be extremely difficult and expensive to resolve. Undisclosed liens become your problem. Improperly recorded deeds can cloud your ownership. And a property acquired without proper legal review may not serve the domicile purpose you intended.

### Section 6 (1 page)

## When to Hire an Attorney vs. When Your CPA Is Enough

Not everything requires a lawyer. Here is a straightforward breakdown of which professional handles which planning needs.

**Your CPA handles:**
- Act 60 decree application and compliance
- PR and federal income tax filing
- Tax optimization and investment structuring
- Annual tax planning and reporting
- Tax consequences of trust funding and distributions
- Presence test documentation and compliance

**Your attorney handles:**
- Trust creation and structuring under Law 219-2012
- Estate plan drafting (wills, powers of attorney, health care directives)
- Forced heirship analysis and compliance
- Domicile documentation and legal evidence preparation
- Decree succession planning
- Property purchase due diligence and closing
- Business entity formation and governance under PR law
- Notarial services (required for deeds, trusts, and certain legal documents)

**Both professionals coordinate on:**
- Tax implications of estate planning strategies
- Trust funding decisions (which assets, when, and how)
- Entity structuring for tax and asset protection purposes
- Federal estate tax exemption planning
- Annual review of combined tax and legal strategy

The bottom line: your CPA and your attorney should be in regular communication. The best outcomes for Act 60 holders come when both professionals understand the full picture.
