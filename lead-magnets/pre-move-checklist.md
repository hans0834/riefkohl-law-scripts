## Who This Checklist Is For

You are considering relocating to Puerto Rico under Act 60 — or you have already committed to the move. Your CPA has outlined the tax benefits. Your relocation consultant has identified neighborhoods and schools.

But have you addressed the legal side?

Puerto Rico is a civil law jurisdiction. Your mainland will, your trust, your business entity, and your estate plan were all built for common law states. Moving to Puerto Rico without updating your legal foundation creates risks that can cost far more than the taxes you save.

This checklist covers the 12 legal steps every Act 60 relocatee should complete — organized by timeline so you know exactly what to do and when.

---

## PHASE 1: Before You Move (3-6 Months Out)

---

### Step 1: Domicile Analysis

**What it is:** A legal assessment of your current domicile status and a strategy for establishing bona fide Puerto Rico domicile — the legal foundation for your Act 60 benefits.

**Why it matters:** Your Act 60 decree is only valid if you are a bona fide resident of Puerto Rico. The IRS evaluates domicile based on objective factors: where you vote, where your driver's license is issued, where your closest social and economic ties are. If your domicile change is challenged, your entire decree — and every tax benefit that came with it — is at risk.

**What happens if you skip it:** You may inadvertently maintain mainland domicile indicators (bank accounts, memberships, voter registration) that give the IRS grounds to challenge your residency. Many decree holders lose audit battles not because they weren't physically present in PR, but because they never formally severed mainland ties.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Inventory all current domicile indicators (voter registration, driver's license, vehicle registration, bank accounts, club memberships, religious organization, professional associations)
- [ ] Develop a domicile severance plan for each mainland indicator
- [ ] Create a timeline for establishing PR domicile indicators upon arrival

---

### Step 2: Mainland Estate Plan Review

**What it is:** A comprehensive review of your existing estate plan — wills, trusts, powers of attorney, beneficiary designations, business succession plans — to identify conflicts with Puerto Rico civil law.

**Why it matters:** Puerto Rico uses civil law, not common law. This affects virtually every aspect of estate planning. Forced heirship rules may require that a portion of your estate go to your children regardless of your will's instructions. Community property rules differ from mainland states. Your revocable trust may not function as intended under PR law.

**What happens if you skip it:** Your mainland estate plan continues to govern by default — but it may be partially or entirely unenforceable in Puerto Rico. At best, this creates costly probate complications. At worst, your assets are distributed contrary to your wishes.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Gather all existing estate documents (wills, trusts, powers of attorney, health care directives, beneficiary designations)
- [ ] Schedule a review with a PR-licensed estate planning attorney
- [ ] Identify conflicts between your current plan and PR civil law
- [ ] Determine whether you need new PR documents, amendments, or a complete restructure

---

### Step 3: Entity Structure Planning

**What it is:** Designing the right business entity structure for your PR-based operations, optimized for your specific decree type (Individual Investor, Export Services, or both).

**Why it matters:** The entity you use to operate your business in Puerto Rico directly affects your decree qualification, your tax treatment, and your liability protection. An LLC formed through an online filing service with a generic operating agreement will not address decree-specific requirements, PR-specific LLC provisions, or the interaction between your entity and your tax strategy.

**What happens if you skip it:** The wrong entity structure can disqualify you from an Export Services decree, create unfavorable tax elections, or leave gaps in liability protection. Fixing a bad entity structure after the fact costs 2-3 times what proper formation costs from the start.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Determine which decree type(s) you are pursuing
- [ ] Analyze whether existing mainland entities can be used or whether new PR entities are needed
- [ ] Evaluate LLC vs. corporation vs. partnership for your specific situation
- [ ] Plan tax elections in coordination with your CPA
- [ ] Identify any entity restructuring needed before the move

---

### Step 4: Pre-Move Appreciation Documentation

**What it is:** Establishing the fair market value of your assets — especially investment portfolios, cryptocurrency, real estate, and business interests — as of the date you establish bona fide PR residency.

**Why it matters:** Under Act 60, capital gains on assets acquired before you become a PR resident are subject to federal taxation on the pre-move appreciation. Only post-move appreciation qualifies for PR's preferential tax rates. If you cannot document the value of your assets at the time of your move, the IRS will make its own determination — and it will not be in your favor.

**What happens if you skip it:** Without a documented valuation as of your residency change date, you have no evidence to separate pre-move gains (federally taxable) from post-move gains (PR taxable at preferential rates). In an audit, this ambiguity almost always resolves against the taxpayer.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Obtain account statements for all investment accounts as of your planned move date
- [ ] Commission formal appraisals for real estate, business interests, and other illiquid assets
- [ ] Document cryptocurrency holdings with blockchain-verified valuations
- [ ] Create a master asset schedule with values as of your residency change date
- [ ] Store documentation securely — you may need it for decades

## PHASE 2: During the Move (First 90 Days)

---

### Step 5: Puerto Rico Will Execution

**What it is:** Executing a new last will and testament that complies with Puerto Rico civil law, or confirming that your existing will is enforceable here.

**Why it matters:** PR has specific requirements for will execution (number of witnesses, notarization, language) that differ from mainland states. More importantly, your will must address forced heirship — the PR legal requirement that reserves a portion of your estate for your descendants. A mainland will that leaves everything to your spouse may be partially overridden by PR law.

**What happens if you skip it:** If you die with only a mainland will, it must be probated under PR rules. Forced heirship will apply regardless of what your will says, potentially creating a contested estate and distribution outcomes you never intended.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Execute a PR-compliant will with a PR-licensed attorney
- [ ] Address forced heirship in the will's dispositive provisions
- [ ] Update beneficiary designations on life insurance, retirement accounts, and financial accounts
- [ ] Execute new powers of attorney and health care directives valid under PR law
- [ ] Store originals securely and provide copies to your attorney

---

### Step 6: Domicile Change Documentation

**What it is:** Creating a comprehensive paper trail that proves you have changed your legal domicile from your mainland state to Puerto Rico.

**Why it matters:** The IRS evaluates domicile based on the totality of circumstances. No single factor is determinative, but the more objective evidence you have, the stronger your position. This documentation is your first line of defense in any challenge to your Act 60 benefits.

**What happens if you skip it:** Without contemporaneous documentation, you are reconstructing evidence years later during an audit — a much weaker position. Decree holders who cannot demonstrate a clean domicile change are the ones most likely to face adverse IRS outcomes.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Obtain a Puerto Rico driver's license
- [ ] Register to vote in Puerto Rico
- [ ] Register vehicles in Puerto Rico
- [ ] Open PR bank accounts and establish primary banking relationship
- [ ] Update your address with the IRS (Form 8822)
- [ ] Cancel or transfer mainland memberships, subscriptions, and affiliations
- [ ] File a declaration of domicile if your former state has one
- [ ] Document everything with dates and keep copies

---

### Step 7: Property Search Legal Review

**What it is:** Legal due diligence on any real property purchase in Puerto Rico, including title search, lien analysis, and compliance with your decree's property requirements.

**Why it matters:** Many Act 60 decrees require the purchase of real property within a specified timeframe. PR property law has nuances that mainland buyers rarely anticipate: different title insurance practices, municipal tax obligations (CRIM), condominium law differences, and potential issues with property registrations. Buying without legal review can result in title defects, unexpected liabilities, or a purchase that doesn't satisfy your decree requirement.

**What happens if you skip it:** Title defects in PR can take years to resolve. CRIM obligations can accumulate penalties. And if your property purchase doesn't meet your decree's requirements, you may need to purchase again — doubling your investment and timeline.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Engage a PR attorney for title search and due diligence before signing a purchase agreement
- [ ] Verify the property satisfies your decree's real property requirement (if applicable)
- [ ] Confirm CRIM (property tax) status — ensure no outstanding obligations transfer to you
- [ ] Review condo documents if applicable (PR condo law differs from mainland)
- [ ] Coordinate closing with your attorney

---

### Step 8: Business Formation and Registration

**What it is:** Formally incorporating or organizing your PR business entity, filing with the Department of State, obtaining your EIN, and registering with applicable agencies.

**Why it matters:** This is not just a filing — it is the legal structure that your decree, your tax strategy, and your operations will run through. The operating agreement must address PR-specific provisions, decree qualification requirements, and the tax election that your CPA has recommended. A generic formation will miss all of these.

**What happens if you skip it:** Operating without a properly formed entity exposes you to personal liability. Operating with a poorly formed entity can jeopardize your decree application or create tax inefficiencies that compound every year.

**Action items:**
- [ ] File articles of organization/incorporation with the PR Department of State
- [ ] Draft and execute a PR-specific operating agreement or bylaws
- [ ] Obtain an EIN from the IRS
- [ ] Register with CRIM (Centro de Recaudacion de Ingresos Municipales)
- [ ] Register with the PR Department of Treasury (Hacienda)
- [ ] Open business bank accounts in Puerto Rico
- [ ] Set up nexus documentation from day one (office lease, employee records, operational logs)

---

### Step 9: Bank Account Setup

**What it is:** Establishing personal and business banking relationships in Puerto Rico to support both your domicile documentation and your business operations.

**Why it matters:** PR bank accounts serve dual purposes: they are strong evidence of domicile change, and they are operationally necessary for running a PR-based business. The IRS looks at where you bank as one factor in evaluating residency. Additionally, certain Act 60 compliance requirements (charitable donations, payroll) are easier to document with PR-based accounts.

**What happens if you skip it:** Continuing to bank primarily on the mainland undercuts your domicile claim and makes compliance documentation harder. Some CPAs have reported that the IRS specifically asks about banking relationships during Act 60 audits.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Open personal checking and savings accounts at a PR bank
- [ ] Open business accounts for each PR entity
- [ ] Transition primary banking activity (payroll, business income, personal expenses) to PR accounts
- [ ] Maintain mainland accounts only as needed (e.g., for mainland rental income or remaining ties)
- [ ] Set up credit card accounts with a PR billing address

## PHASE 3: After You Settle (First Year)

---

### Step 10: Trust Creation

**What it is:** Establishing an irrevocable trust under Puerto Rico Law 219-2012, funded with appropriate assets, and structured to work alongside your Act 60 decree.

**Why it matters:** A properly structured PR trust accomplishes what no other single tool can: asset protection, estate tax reduction, forced heirship compliance, and decree succession planning — all within one legal instrument. For decree holders with significant assets, a trust is not optional; it is the centerpiece of a sound legal plan.

**What happens if you skip it:** Without a trust, your assets remain in your personal estate, exposed to creditors, subject to PR forced heirship at death (possibly in unintended ways), and without a mechanism for decree succession. You are leaving both protection and tax savings on the table.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Determine the appropriate trust type (irrevocable vs. revocable) based on your goals
- [ ] Select and appoint a trustee (individual or institutional)
- [ ] Draft the trust instrument with a PR-licensed trust attorney
- [ ] Execute the trust with PR-compliant formalities
- [ ] Fund the trust (transfer designated assets into the trust)
- [ ] Coordinate trust tax elections with your CPA
- [ ] Establish trust administration procedures and record-keeping

---

### Step 11: Annual Compliance System

**What it is:** Building a year-round system for tracking and meeting every Act 60 compliance obligation — presence test documentation, charitable donations, employment requirements, DDEC reporting, and tax filing deadlines.

**Why it matters:** Act 60 compliance is not a once-a-year event. It requires continuous attention throughout the year. The consequences of non-compliance are severe: decree revocation, back taxes, penalties, and interest. A system that tracks every obligation, provides quarterly checkpoints, and flags approaching deadlines is the difference between stress-free compliance and a year-end scramble.

**What happens if you skip it:** You rely on memory and ad hoc tracking. Deadlines get missed. Documentation gaps accumulate. By the time you realize there is a problem — often at tax filing time — it may be too late to fix.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Download or create your annual compliance calendar (see our 2026 Act 60 Compliance Calendar)
- [ ] Set up a presence test tracking system (spreadsheet, app, or log)
- [ ] Schedule charitable donations to meet your decree's annual requirement
- [ ] Set quarterly review dates with your attorney and CPA
- [ ] Create a filing system for compliance documentation (digital and physical)
- [ ] Set calendar reminders for every major deadline

---

### Step 12: Professional Team Assembly

**What it is:** Building a coordinated team of PR-based professionals — attorney, CPA, financial advisor, insurance agent, and relocation consultant — who communicate with each other and understand the Act 60 framework.

**Why it matters:** Act 60 planning sits at the intersection of tax law, estate law, business law, and financial planning. No single professional covers all of it. The most successful decree holders have a team where the CPA and attorney coordinate, the financial advisor understands the trust structure, and everyone is working from the same plan.

**What happens if you skip it:** You get siloed advice. Your CPA recommends a trust without understanding the legal implications. Your mainland attorney drafts documents that don't work under PR law. Your financial advisor makes investment decisions without considering the trust's provisions. The result is an expensive, inconsistent patchwork that leaves gaps.

**Action items:**
- [ ] Confirm you have a PR-licensed attorney with Act 60 and trust expertise
- [ ] Confirm you have a CPA experienced in Act 60 compliance and PR tax law
- [ ] Identify a PR-based financial advisor who understands Act 60 investment structures
- [ ] Secure PR-based insurance coverage (health, property, liability)
- [ ] Introduce your team members to each other and establish communication protocols
- [ ] Schedule an annual team meeting to review your overall plan
